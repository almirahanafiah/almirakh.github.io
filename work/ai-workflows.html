Staying in control of an AI that doesn't stop.

01 · Overview

Overview

At Brim I lead the design of how the product communicates with people. In practice that means notifications, approvals, ambient state, and the desktop app. Brim itself is an AI system that helps mid-sized businesses own their specific intelligence, which is to say the way their company actually works and the patterns that make it good at what it does.

The most interesting part of this work, for me, has been the gap between what the AI does in the background and what the user actually feels is happening. This case study is about how I came to believe most of the design work was really about keeping the human in control of that gap.

02 · TL;DR

TL;DR

For the recruiter who skims, the whole story in three lines.

The challenge

Across roughly 100 user interviews, the loudest concern people had about AI was about being replaced. They kept asking whether AI would take over their job, and whether it would do everything for them without asking first. Security and compliance handle the data side of that. What I was working on was the layer that sits on top of it, where the user actually feels whether they're in control.

What I did

Defined a small set of interaction principles around quiet ambient presence, intentional interruption, inspectability of every action, and human-in-the-loop wherever the consequence is high. Then I worked with engineering to ship those principles across in-product surfaces and the desktop app, so the same logic carries through wherever the user encounters the product.

The outcome

A consistent notification and ambient-state language now lives across 50+ surfaces. The product is judged by what it handles without bothering anyone, and no consequential action ever ships without a person saying yes to it first.

03 · Problem

The problem

From roughly 100 user interviews, the fear surfaced again and again: “Will AI just take over my job? Will it do everything for me?”

Most concerns about AI get framed as concerns about data, and that makes sense. Security and compliance handle that layer through work like SOC 2, audit, and data residency. What I was working on sits on top of that, in the experience layer, where the user actually feels whether they're in control. The interface is where data security gets expressed back to the user, where the abstract guarantee becomes something they can see and trust.

People weren't only asking whether their information was safe. They were also asking whether they were still needed once the AI was running things in the background. That kind of fear is something only the interface can answer, which means a single notification component won't do. It has to be a whole system of behaviours, designed around keeping the human at the centre.

04 · Research

Research

The signal came up over and over again. Across interviews with operators, managers, and executives, the dominant fear was about replacement. People wanted to know whether they were still needed once the AI was running things on their behalf. Underneath that bigger question, more specific worries started to cluster: what data is it using, what is it doing right now, when will it ask me, what happens if it gets something wrong, and can I see what it did?

05 · Principles

Principles

Five principles that direct how every surface is designed. Together they describe how the system behaves and earns trust.

01

The human has the final say.

Every consequential action waits for a human yes. The AI can prepare, propose, and queue things up, but it does not commit on its own. This is the load-bearing rule I kept coming back to, and the other principles all descend from it.

02

Agents post like colleagues, not systems that message.

Every action that surfaces is attributed to a named agent, with an avatar and a name, like a teammate posting in Slack. The user reads the activity the way they'd read a team feed. Framing the agents as colleagues is one of the design moves I'm most proud of, because it answers the replacement fear in the most direct way I had available.

03

Trust is verified, not told.

Every action carries a source, a reason, a confidence number, and a timestamp. The user can open any moment and see what happened, and why. Trust comes from being able to look at the work itself, rather than from being told it's fine.

04

Quiet by default; honest when the system needs you.

The ambient layer holds steady when nothing requires attention. When an action genuinely needs the human and hasn't been picked up, the system says so directly, with something like “You haven't weighed in on this yet, so I'm bringing it back up.” I wanted that honesty to feel like a colleague being upfront with you.

05

One event, one truth, every surface.

A single event renders across many surfaces: in-product, the desktop, and email. The discipline is that they all carry the same title, the same severity, and the same source. What the user saw on the lock screen is what they see when they open the app a minute later.

06 · Solution

The solution

The five principles together produce a system of behaviours rather than a single notification component. Three of those behaviours are worth showing here.

01 · The ambient layer

A thin persistent presence at the top of the product, streaming what the system is doing in real time. It pulses gently when something arrives, holds quietly when nothing does, and collapses to a single mark when the user wants the volume turned down. The system keeps working in the background, and the user gets to control how much they hear about it. I imagined this as the kind of background hum a person would feel rather than read.

02 · Agents as colleagues

Most AI products surface activity as system notifications, with copy along the lines of “Your invoice was processed.” That puts the AI in front of the user, and quietly reinforces the fear. I designed the activity stream as a feed of colleagues instead. Every action is attributed to a named agent with an avatar, like a teammate posting in Slack. The user becomes the manager of the team rather than a recipient of notifications, and that small shift changes how the whole product reads.

03 · Cross-surface continuity

When the user is outside the product, like in a different tool or away from their machine, the same logic carries through. The desktop notification holds the same voice as the in-product surface, hands off without losing context, and stays an ambient note instead of an alarm. Trust really does break the moment a notification on the lock screen disagrees with what's in the app.

In practice, a single attention event in this product can show up across roughly ten different surfaces. Each one has a different tier of attention, a different density, and a different shape. What ties them together is the discipline that the title, the severity, and the source stay coherent everywhere they appear.

Beyond Brim

None of these principles are really Brim-specific. They describe a shape that any AI product acting on a user's behalf is going to need, which is more or less every tool we'll be using over the next few years.

07 · Learnings

Learnings

What worked

Naming the human has the final say as the load-bearing principle simplified arguments inside the team in a way I didn't expect. When a feature proposal felt off, it was almost always a violation of that rule, and pointing back to it was easier than relitigating every notification one by one.

What I'd do differently

In hindsight I'd have spent more time thinking about how each user actually speaks, and worked that into the onboarding. If the notifications were seeded from the user's own tone and the way they communicate, the whole experience would feel much more personal and cohesive. The colleague framing only really lands when the colleague sounds something like the user themselves.
