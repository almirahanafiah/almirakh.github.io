Bootcamp case study, 2024

From a social feed to a personal curator.

A 12-week project on personal style that began as a Pinterest-style inspiration feed and ended up, after a second round of user testing, as an AI-curated styling tool built around the clothes a person already owns.

01 · Overview

Overview

GAIA is a personal-style app, designed over 12 weeks for my UX bootcamp capstone. The brief was open, find a human-centred problem to design for, and the one I kept coming back to was my own. I loved clothes, and I wanted to figure out whether there was a way to dress more from what I already owned and less from what was being sold to me.

The shape of the work moved twice. A first round of interviews led me to design a social inspiration feed as the MVP, and a second round of usability testing pulled it somewhere different, toward a personal curator built around the user's wardrobe. That second turn is what most of this case study is about.

02 · TL;DR

TL;DR

For the recruiter who skims, the whole story in three lines.

The challenge

Many people aspire to a personal style that reflects who they are, but the way style is sold to them, through fast fashion, micro-trends, and a noisy social feed, often gets in the way of figuring out what they actually like.

What I did

Ran user interviews on personal style, designed a social inspiration feed as the MVP, tested it across two rounds, then redesigned around an idea that came out of testing, an AI curator that builds outfits from the clothes someone already owns.

The outcome

A full design from interviews to a high-fidelity prototype, the GAIA brand, and a clear sense of where the project wanted to go next, toward AI-curated outfits drawn from a person's own wardrobe and linked out to the brands they already love.

03 · Problem

The problem

Personal style is hard to hear when so much of what surrounds you is built to sell you a trend.

For me, this was something I felt personally. I had spent a lot of time buying things I didn't wear and scrolling through outfits without feeling much closer to my own. The question I wanted the project to sit with was whether a digital tool could help someone find their own taste, rather than push them further from it.

04 · Research

Research

I ran six user interviews with women aged 22 to 31, who all shopped online and used social media for style. I kept the questions open, around how they described their style, what frustrated them about shopping, and what they wanted a tool to help with.

Key insight, from the interviews

All six interviewees said the same thing: it's much easier to figure out whether a piece of clothing will work for you when you see it on someone who looks like you, in skin tone, body shape, and the way they hold themselves. That finding shaped the first version of the app.

After two rounds of usability testing with five users each, the design was working. What I hadn't expected was the kind of question that came up in round two, which kept circling back to the same gap and pointed somewhere I hadn't planned for.

Key insight, from the testing

Two of the five testers asked variations of the same question: could you shop from this, and could it suggest outfits made from clothes you already own. Neither was on the build list, but both echoed what I had heard in the interviews. That gap was where the project shifted from a social feed to a personal curator.

05 · Ideation

Ideation

With the interview findings in hand, I worked through about forty user stories across three epics: wardrobe organisation, personalised style ideas, and social style discovery. Tara's biggest frustration was how long it took to find plus-size influencers who dressed the way she wanted, so the home feed had to filter by physical traits before she scrolled. A competitive scan pointed me toward a gap, somewhere on the socially-oriented and visually simple end. The interviews told me social media was already where most people found style inspiration, and the friction was mostly about how cluttered the feed felt. So a Pinterest-style feed, with a cleaner shape and a style quiz at onboarding, felt like the right MVP.

06 · Solution

The solution

Two moments, the MVP and the iteration that came out of testing.

01 · The first version, GAIA as a social feed

The first version of GAIA was a fashion inspiration feed built as four surfaces that fed each other: a home page filtered by the user's physical traits and brand preferences from the style quiz, a personal wardrobe they could upload their own clothes into, item and outfit detail pages, and collections to organise outfits by mood and occasion. Underneath, I built a small UI library so the prototype shipped consistently, with a 3:1 contrast minimum on interactive elements.

02 · The personal curator

The second direction came out of those testing questions and reframed the wardrobe. What had been a side feature became the starting point, with an AI styling assistant that built outfits from the user's wardrobe, suggested items from high-street and small brands, and explained itself by referencing the user's onboarding answers. Each suggestion came with a short note underneath, like built from your navy blazer and styled around the looks you saved from your favourite influencer, so it read as a recommendation rather than a verdict.

07 · Learnings

Learnings

What worked

Staying close to testing was the most useful thing I did. The second round was meant to validate the design and ended up reshaping it, and the work was better for letting that happen.

What I'd do differently

I'd test earlier, on paper or with a rough prototype. The questions in round two were close enough to interview signal that earlier testing could have caught the direction sooner.
