From a social feed to a personal stylist.

Redesigned a conceptual personal-style app from a social inspiration feed into an AI stylist that builds outfits from the clothes you already own, solving for personalised styling with the least possible friction.

01 · Overview

Overview

GAIA is a personal-style app I designed over twelve weeks, first as my UX bootcamp capstone and then through a short iteration once I realised where it really needed to go. The brief was open, find a human-centred problem worth designing for, and the one I kept circling back to was my own. I love clothes, and I wanted to know whether a tool could help someone dress more from what they already owned and a little less from whatever was being sold to them that week.

The interesting part of this project is not the final screens, it is the turn in the middle. Coming from a background in social media marketing, I set out to build a better place to find style inspiration, a feed, and my research seemed to back me up. Presenting the first version, I realised I had built the wrong thing, and that I had been designing for the app I wanted to exist. Most of this case study is about that turn, and what I did with it.

02 · TL;DR

TL;DR

The challenge

People want a personal style that feels like theirs, but personalised styling help is expensive and out of reach, so they lean on social media and impulse-buy trend pieces that do not suit them. The real need was an affordable, personalised stylist for the clothes they already own.

What I did

I ran interviews and built a social inspiration feed as the first version. Presenting it, I caught that I had leaned into my own bias toward a social product, and that people were not asking for another feed. I reframed the problem around personalised outfit ideas with the least possible friction, and rebuilt GAIA as an AI styling tool, then tested the new direction with five users.

The outcome

GAIA became an AI stylist that reads what you ask for, matches it to your style profile, and builds a full outfit from your own wardrobe, suggesting new pieces to buy where they would help. In testing it felt more useful, and more fun, than another app to doomscroll.

03 · Research

What people told me, and how I read it.

I ran six interviews with women aged 22 to 31 who shopped online and used social media for style, kept the questions open, and pulled secondary research alongside them. The problem space that came back was clear, and a little uncomfortable.

The problem

People want a personal style that reflects who they are, but personalised styling guidance is expensive and hard to come by. A professional stylist is a luxury, so people lean on fast fashion and social media instead, impulse-buying trend pieces that do not suit them and ending up with cluttered wardrobes full of clothes they cannot style.

My interviews sharpened where the gap really was. People were not asking to be sold more clothes, they wanted help styling what they already owned, and a few said outright they would take a stylist in a heartbeat if one were affordable.

From my primary research

Most people look to social media to find style inspiration rather than wanting a professional stylist. But many are frustrated by the lack of personalisation when they try to style what they already own.

Secondary research backed the stakes: 89% of Gen-Z shoppers said fashion boosts their confidence, 65% said choosing an outfit is the most stressful part of going out, and 46% had wanted a trend but could not find it in their size. To keep the work grounded in a person, I built a persona, Tara, around the frustration I heard most often.

Here is where my bias did the work. The research was pointing at a stylist, just an affordable and accessible one. I reached for a feed, because a better inspiration feed was the answer I already wanted. I heard that people use social media for style and skipped the harder half, that what they needed was the styling itself.

The research pointed at a stylist. I reached for a feed.
04 · Version one

A cleaner, more personal feed.

The first version of GAIA was a social inspiration feed. Onboarding was a style quiz, the home was a feed of looks on people who dressed and looked like you, you could keep a personal wardrobe of your own clothes, and you could style around an item you owned and save the result to a collection.

The style quiz did real work

I tried to make the quiz earn its place rather than just collect taps. It doubled as a colour-palette test, so someone came away knowing which colours actually suited them, and it took rough body measurements to map them to a Kibbe body type, so the app understood their proportions before it ever recommended anything.

The feed, from wireframe to brand

In usability testing people moved through it easily and liked how it looked. Nothing was broken on the surface. What was wrong sat underneath, in what the app was actually for.

05 · The turn

Presenting it, I realised I had built the wrong thing.

The penny dropped when I started showing the first version. The questions I kept getting back were not about the feed at all. People asked whether GAIA could just tell them what to wear with what they already had, and whether they could shop the looks they saw. I had built a beautiful way to find inspiration, when the thing people actually needed was the styling itself, the part a stylist does that a feed never could. I had drifted from my own research. People already use social media and will keep using it whether or not I make a gentler version, so a nicer feed was never going to close the gap. They needed the affordable, personalised styling the work had pointed at the whole time.

The feed needed everyone else to do the work

Seeing style on people who own what you own only works once thousands of other people have uploaded and tagged their clothes. That is a mountain of steps and a cold start, a lot of friction before the app is useful to anyone.

The quiz gathered data and gave nothing back

The style quiz was a useful signal, but on its own it just collected information and handed the styling work back to the user. The personalisation needed to resolve into an actual outfit, and quickly.

The real problem was always an affordable, personalised stylist for the clothes you already own.
06 · Version two

Reframing GAIA from a feed into a stylist.

An affordable, personalised stylist is exactly what AI made possible, and it is the two-week iteration that turned GAIA into what it should have been.

With AI, the hard parts got easy. It can build a full outfit from the clothes you own, whether you upload them or add them from a database of pieces, which quietly removes the friction of photographing your whole wardrobe. It can personalise the look to your style profile, and it can suggest the new items that would complete it, each with a link to buy, which is where the commercial side lives.

A new home, built around what helps

The first thing I changed was the home. Instead of a feed to scroll, it opens on the things that actually help, the style assistant, your style profile, and a quick way to add to your wardrobe, then below them, what to wear today based on your weather and location, a full fit idea generated from your wardrobe, and a browse-inspiration section. I did not throw the social part away, I changed where the effort sat. Rather than asking people to upload and tag their outfits, I imagined the inspiration pulling from the Instagram and social accounts they already post to, so it costs them nothing to take part.

The styling assistant

The heart of v2 is the styling assistant. You can tap a shortcut, style by occasion, by aesthetic, or start with a centre piece, or you can just type the way you would text a friend, something casual and feminine for brunch tomorrow. The assistant reads the keywords in what you said and checks them first against your style profile, your undertones, your colour palette, your Kibbe body type, even your personality, before it builds the outfit, a top, a bottom, outerwear and shoes, and accessories when the look needs them.

When it gets it wrong

I did not want the assistant to feel like a verdict, because an outfit is personal and the AI will not always get it right. So when a suggestion misses, you can ask it to try again with a note on what to change, or swap any single piece for something else in your wardrobe or a quick web search, whichever is faster. Every item is labelled as something you already own or something new, so you always know what a look would really cost you.

What I kept, and what I evolved

Kept
  • A personal wardrobe of your own clothes
  • The style profile built from the quiz
  • A thread of social inspiration to browse
Evolved
  • A feed to scroll became a stylist that answers
  • Outfits got generated, instead of hunted for
  • Photos came from a database and your own social, not a manual upload

Testing the new direction

I put the new flow in front of five users and asked each of them to get an outfit for something real in their week. The difference in effort was the clearest signal. Version one only became useful once a user, and everyone else, had uploaded and tagged their clothes, where version two gets you a full, wearable outfit from a single sentence. It landed as far more compelling, and honestly more fun, than another app to doomscroll, which was the whole point.

07 · Learnings

What I took from it.

The big one

Design for the latent need, the thing people are circling but not asking for directly. Bolting a feature onto a product that already exists, a slightly better feed, is rarely worth switching for and is easily replaced. Building something genuinely new is what earns a place in someone's day.

What worked

Listening to the part of my research that did not flatter my idea. The turn was uncomfortable, and the project was much better for letting it happen.

What I'd do differently

I would separate what I wanted to build from what the research was telling me far earlier, and narrow in on the why before I let myself fall in love with a what.
